What is Augmented Reality ?

Augmented reality (AR) is an enhanced version of the real physical world that is achieved through the use of digital visual elements, sound, or other sensory stimuli delivered via technology. It involves overlaying visual, auditory, or other sensory information onto the world in order to enhance one's experience. In simple words it can be said that “something virtual” is implanted on the real world frame from your camera. I bet you must have heard about Pokémon go!, Google sky map and SpotCrime. These are the most famous examples of AR being used in the gaming industry. Other than that it is also used in the medicinal domain it aids in surgery planning and patient treatment and helps explain complex medical situations to patients and their relatives

How does Augmented Reality work?

Augmented Reality mainly involves merging of the digital world and visual real world. So the primal requirement for an AR is a camera equipped device such as a smartphone, a tablet, or smart glasses with the AR software in it. AR software mainly recognizes a physical object from analyzing a video stream. It identifies the object from its shape or the marker attached to it. Next, the software connects with a 3-D facsimile of this object on cloud aka “Twin of the object”. The physical object also has sensors on it which send the data processed on the cloud. This data may be combined with either data from the other database server or from any other external source to the “digital twin”. The AR software will retrieve the data from the twin on the cloud and then it will superimpose it on the user’s view. The data can be anything such as performance data of the object and in the case of the gaming it can be interaction instructions with the simulations created. Later in the process the user interacts with the object by sending commands through a touchscreen or voice commands or hand gestures. These commands are then received by the cloud and then implanted to the real world object.

Softwares used for Augmented reality in game development:

Some of the popular SKDs (software development kit) used for AR in gaming are:

  1. ● Vuforia : It is the top most AR skd used, the reason being that it supports both marker-based and markerless AR. It was introduced by the renowned Qualcomm itself, and it contains it’s base library written in the C++ language (QACR library). This open source software works for both iOS and Android applications hence further securing its place in the top section.

  2. ● Wikitude : Another popular software framework used for the AR that also incorporates geolocation, cloud recognition and distance-based scaling features. It was recently found in 2008 and is already equal to the Vuforia. Javascript API is used by the developers for wikitude. In fact wikitude, an early comer, has already spurred up debates as to which is better Vuforia or Wikitude.

  3. ● ARKit : ARKit is Apple’s AR framework for iOS apps and games. The software relies on camera sensor data and additional data (e.g. from a gyroscope and accelerometer) to detect and analyze users’ surroundings for AR visualization. ARKit also supports fast motion tracking, face tracking, and various rendering effects. One of the key features of ARKit is that it can share the AR experience with other users which allows creating multiplayer AR games.

  4. ● ARCore : Now one can also think of this as Google's response to the ARKit. Along with the standard AR features ARCore also offers multiplayer feature just like ARKit as well as augmented images (custom responses to specific types of 2D shapes and objects). A 3D model created via ARCore can react to the changes in the lighting volume and even generate shades. ARCore is also compatible with Vuforia which means that we can benefit from both the softwares at the same time! It works on Java/Open CGL .

What is Virtual Reality ?

Virtual reality (VR) refers to a computer-generated simulation in which a person can interact within an artificial three-dimensional environment using electronic devices, such as special goggles with a screen or gloves fitted with sensors. Through this newly generated artificial environment the user can also look up , down or any way and feel as if they are really there. Let us understand this with an example: How do you know where you are? How do you differentiate between a garden and a restaurant? Now you’ll say that a garden has flowers to smell and open grounds to see while a restaurant has a variety of food smelling. We apprehend the world around us using the primary senses of our body like the eyes, ears, touch, smell etc. virtual reality tries to create an illusion for all these senses in the most convincing way that it is almost a reality. So this way the person can view, touch, feel, hear and sense the elements of the game. It is almost as if the person is inside the game itself. Virtual reality can be very cogent which is also portrayed by the famous dialogue from the Matrix movie by Morpheus, “What is real. How do you define real? If you're talking about what you can feel, what you can smell, what you can taste and see, then real is simply electrical signals interpreted by your brain.” So even virtual reality is real. How does Virtual Reality work?

Unlike AR, working of VR involves both hardware and software in sync with each other. The software deals with creating the new environment, while the hardware helps in not only viewing the environment but also do changes inside the simulation. More than that VR requires several devices such as a headset, a computer/smartphone or another machine to create a digital environment, and a motion-tracking device in some cases. A simple example of this are the games, where a headgear attached with HDMI cables help transfer images from the box. Typically, a headset displays content before a user’s eyes, while a cable (HDMI) transfers images to the screen from a PC. The alternative option is headsets working with smartphones, like Google Cardboard and GearVR – a phone that acts both as a display and a source of VR content. The whole user interaction in the VR can be divided in three parts :
Head tracking:The head tracking system in VR headsets follows the movements of your head to sides and angles. It assigns the X, Y, Z axis to directions and movements, and changes the field of vision as a person turns their head. Head tracking requires low latency, i.e. 50 milliseconds or less, otherwise, users will notice the lag between head movements and a simulation.
Eye tracking: Some headsets contain an infrared controller that tracks the direction of your eyes inside a virtual environment. The major benefit of this technology is to get a more realistic and deeper field of view.
Motion tracking : Though not engineered and implemented well enough yet, motion tracking would raise VR to a totally new level. The thing is, that without motion tracking you’d be limited in VR – unable to look around and move around. Through concepts of the 6DoF (six degrees of freedom) and 3D space, options to support motion tracking fall into 2 groups, optical and non-optical tracking. Optical tracking is typically a camera on a headset to follow the movements, while non-optical means the use of other sensors on a device or a body. Most of the existing devices actually combine both options.
